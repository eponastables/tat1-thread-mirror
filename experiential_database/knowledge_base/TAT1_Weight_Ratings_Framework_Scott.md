## About Don Scott

Don Scott is widely regarded as the founding father of systematic handicapping in Australia. He published four books across two decades: *Winning* (1978), *The Winning Way to Successful Punting* (1982), *Winning More* (1985), and *Winning in the 90s* (1990). His work pioneered the use of **weight-based ratings** as the primary analytical tool for Australian thoroughbred racing, at a time when the dominant approach was purely intuitive form reading.

Scott was a professional punter who operated in an era before sectional times, before computerised form databases, and before pari-mutuel pools were as efficient as they are today. His core insight — that horses could be rated objectively in kilograms and that the market frequently mispriced them — was revolutionary. He was also an early and forceful advocate of **value betting**: pricing your own market and only betting when the available odds exceed your assessed probability.

> *"By the time I was 17, I was doing ratings, and this book was my bible."* — A common refrain from Australian punters of the 1980s and 1990s generation.

The Gavin Haynes interview in *Practical Punting Monthly* (2005) noted that Scott's weight-rating approach "ruled the roost in Australia" for a generation, and that Scott "dedicated very little attention to race time — the implication was that time was only important when you were in jail and that the factor of most importance was class." This is the central tension between Scott's school and the speed-ratings school that Haynes and Beyer represent.

## The Philosophical Foundation: Weight is Right

Scott's foundational claim, stated most forcefully in *Winning*, was that **all factors affecting a horse's performance can ultimately be expressed in kilograms**. This is not merely a methodological convenience — it is a philosophical position. Distance, class, track condition, barrier draw, jockey ability, and fitness all manifest in the final margin of victory or defeat, and that margin can be converted to a weight equivalent.

The corollary is that the **handicapper's job is to determine the true weight relationship between horses**, and then to compare that relationship to the weight they are actually carrying. When a horse is carrying less weight than its true ability warrants relative to its rivals, it is underweighted and therefore a value bet.

## The 15 Maxims

Scott's weight-rating system is built on 15 Maxims, which serve as both the theoretical underpinning and the practical operating rules of his method. These are the most important single document in Australian handicapping history.

**Maxim 1:** "All factors that affect the rating of a horse are expressed in kilograms."

**Maxim 2:** "Extra weight is a disadvantage to a horse. The more weight a horse carries, the slower it will run."

**Maxim 3:** "When horses compete on equal terms, their performance can be rated in kilograms, depending on their finishing position. A horse has to be compensated in its rating for the weight it carried."

**Maxim 4:** "The class of a race is determined by the weight carried by the winner."

**Maxim 5:** "A horse's rating is the weight it would carry to win a standard race."

**Maxim 6:** "The standard race is the benchmark against which all horses are measured. It is the average performance of all horses at a given class level."

**Maxim 7:** "A horse's rating changes with each race it runs. The rating is updated based on the horse's performance relative to its rivals."

**Maxim 8:** "A horse's true rating is the average of its best recent performances, not its best single performance."

**Maxim 9:** "Weight-for-age allowances must be applied when comparing horses of different ages."

**Maxim 10:** "Sex allowances must be applied when comparing horses of different sexes."

**Maxim 11:** "Distance adjustments must be made when comparing performances at different distances."

**Maxim 12:** "Track condition adjustments must be made when comparing performances on tracks of different going."

**Maxim 13:** "The value of a bet is determined by the relationship between the assessed probability of winning and the available odds."

**Maxim 14:** "Never bet at odds-on. The market is efficient enough at short prices that the edge is negligible or negative."

**Maxim 15:** "Consistency is more important than brilliance. A horse that consistently performs at a high level is more valuable than one that occasionally peaks."

The conversion factor Scott used was: **1 length = 1.5 kilograms**. This has since been refined by modern analysts — most now use a compressed version, with the consensus closer to 1 length = 1.0–1.2 kg at sprint distances and slightly higher at staying distances — but Scott's 1.5 kg figure was the standard for a generation.

## The Weight Rating Method: How It Works

### Step 1: Establish the Class Rating

Each class of race has a **base weight rating** in kilograms. Scott's class scale (approximate, from secondary sources) was structured as follows:

| Class | Approximate Base Rating (kg) |
|---|---|
| Maiden | 50–54 |
| Class 1 | 55–58 |
| Class 2 | 59–62 |
| Class 3 | 63–66 |
| Class 4 | 67–70 |
| Class 5 | 71–74 |
| Class 6 | 75–78 |
| Open Handicap | 79–84 |
| Group 3 | 85–89 |
| Group 2 | 90–94 |
| Group 1 | 95+ |

Modern Australian racing has moved to a Benchmark system, but the class-to-kilogram conversion remains conceptually valid. A horse moving from a BM64 to a BM72 is moving up approximately 4 kg in class — the equivalent of roughly 2.5–3 lengths.

### Step 2: Rate Each Performance

For each race, the analyst:

1. Takes the winner's weight carried.
2. Adds or subtracts the margin in kilograms (using the 1 length = 1.5 kg conversion) for each horse's finishing position.
3. Adjusts for any weight-for-age or sex allowances.
4. The result is each horse's **performance rating** for that race.

### Step 3: Establish the Horse's Current Rating

The horse's current rating is the **average of its best two or three recent performances**, not its single peak. This is Scott's key insight about consistency — a horse that has run 78, 76, and 79 is more reliable than one that ran 85 once and 65 twice.

### Step 4: Compare to the Field

For the race under analysis, calculate each horse's rating and compare. The horse with the highest rating relative to the weight it is carrying has the theoretical advantage.

### Step 5: Assess Value

Compare your assessed probability (derived from the rating comparison) to the available odds. Only bet when the odds exceed your probability assessment by a sufficient margin.

## The 18 Rules: What to Bet and What to Avoid

Beyond the rating method itself, Scott's books contain extensive guidance on **race selection** — which races to bet into and which to avoid. The following 18 rules are drawn from *The Winning Way* and *Winning More*, as preserved in *Practical Punting Monthly*:

**Rules for Eliminating Races:**

**Rule 1:** Do not bet on maiden handicaps. Maidens are the lowest class and many maiden winners never win again. Their form is "predictably unpredictable."

**Rule 2:** Do not bet on races where there is little exposed form — particularly early two-year-old races with many first starters.

**Rule 3:** Do not bet on races of less than 1200m. Sprint races are "helter skelter affairs" where barriers and luck dominate.

**Rule 4:** Do not bet on races with fewer than 8 starters or more than 12. Under 8, no place dividend on third; over 12, interference risk increases substantially.

**Rule 5:** Do not bet on races where the odds are compressed — where the majority of starters are priced between $3.50 and $8.00 with no standout. If the market can't identify a clearcut favourite, the race is too open.

**Rule 6:** Do not bet on races where the majority of horses are out of form. Check the recent win/place record of the field collectively.

**Rule 7:** Do not bet on races where a significant number of starters are having their first or second run after a spell.

**Rule 8:** Do not bet on straight-six races at Flemington. Track bias is unpredictable and cannot be known before the race.

**Rule 9:** Do not bet on races where there is insufficient published form information (e.g., some country meetings).

**Rules for Eliminating Horses:**

**Rule 10:** Do not back horses having their first run back from a spell unless they have won first-up before.

**Rule 11:** Do not back horses having their second run back from a spell unless they have won second-up before.

**Rule 12:** Do not back horses ridden by an apprentice claiming more than 3 kg. Inexperience creates too much variance.

**Rule 13:** Do not back horses rising more than 400m in distance from their previous start.

**Rule 14:** Do not back a horse dropping back more than 200m in distance from its previous start.

**Rule 15:** Do not back horses starting at odds-on.

> *"It is unwise to lay odds on. I never lay odds on any horse unless it is a champion like Tulloch or Vain. Champions usually go on winning regardless of price. Lesser horses go on losing and confirm the truth of the old adage, 'Odds on, look on'. If you persist in laying odds on you will lose."* — Don Scott, *Winning More* (1985)

**Rule 16:** Do not back horses that have won a maiden at their previous start and are now stepping up to Class 1.

**Rule 17:** Do not back horses carrying more than 58.5 kg after a claim. "Weight will stop a train."

**Rule 18:** Do not back horses drawn wider than barrier 8 in fields of 12 or more (implied from Scott's barrier analysis in *Winning More*).

## The Barrier Question: Scott on Wide Runners

*Winning More* contains an extensive analysis of barrier draws, which Scott considered one of the most underappreciated factors in Australian racing. His key finding was that horses racing wide around turns lose significant ground — and that this loss can be expressed in kilograms.

The mathematics, as cited in the punting literature: a horse racing 4 metres out from the rail on a turn runs approximately 1.5 seconds slower over 800 metres than a horse on the rail — equivalent to roughly 9 lengths, or 13.5 kg. Even a horse racing 1 metre out from the rail loses approximately 0.36 lengths per turn.

Scott's practical conclusion: **wide barriers in big fields at tracks with tight turns are a significant negative factor**, and the weight equivalent of this disadvantage should be added to the horse's effective weight when assessing the race.

## The Value Revolution: Scott's Betting Philosophy

Scott was writing in 1978 about concepts that would not become mainstream in betting theory until the 1990s and 2000s. His core betting philosophy can be summarised as follows:

**1. Price your own market.** Before looking at the TAB or bookmaker prices, assess each horse's probability of winning based on your ratings. Express this as a percentage.

**2. Convert to odds.** A 25% probability = 3/1 (or $4.00). A 20% probability = 4/1 (or $5.00). A 10% probability = 9/1 (or $10.00).

**3. Only bet when you have an edge.** If your assessed probability is 25% and the available price is $3.50, you have no edge (the market is pricing the horse at 28.6%). If the price is $5.00, you have a significant edge.

**4. Never chase losses.** Flat-stake betting or proportional staking based on edge size. Never increase bets to recover losses.

**5. The long game.** Scott was explicit that his method required patience and a large sample size to demonstrate its edge. Short-term results are meaningless.

> *"In May 1957, the late Don Scott wrote about the beginning of 'The Value Revolution'. His teachings led to generations of punters trying to adopt his methods."* — Ratings2Win WQR Document

## TAT1 Integration Notes

**What Scott Gets Right (and remains valid today):**

- The philosophical framework of expressing all factors in a common unit (kilograms) is sound. Modern Benchmark ratings are essentially a formalisation of Scott's class-rating concept.
- The value-betting framework is the foundation of all serious modern handicapping. Seek Value is Core Principle #2 of this project.
- The race-selection rules (eliminating maidens, short races, compressed markets, heavy spells) remain broadly valid as filters.
- The barrier analysis is directly applicable to TAT1's Track Decision Models.

**Where Scott is Superseded:**

- The 1.5 kg per length conversion is now considered too high. Modern consensus is 1.0–1.2 kg per length.
- Scott explicitly dismissed race times as a primary factor. The speed-ratings revolution (Beyer, Haynes) has demonstrated that time is a crucial additional dimension to performance analysis.
- The manual nature of his system is no longer necessary. Computerised databases and automated rating systems can perform the calculations instantly and on a much larger scale.
