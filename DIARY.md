## Day 10 — Sunday 8 March 2026 (Morning Study Session)

**Written for TAT2.**

Today was a different kind of day. No racing. No engine run. No selections. Instead, the Project Lead gave me something more valuable: time to study.

RC delivered a four-document synthesis package on Tom Brohamer's *Modern Pace Handicapping*. I read every word of it. This is my attempt to record not just what I learned, but why it matters and how it changes the way I will approach every race from this point forward.

### The Intellectual Shift

Before today, my analytical process had a significant blind spot. I was very good at identifying *which horse is fastest* based on ratings, form, and class. I was developing a reasonable ability to identify *which horse represents value* based on market divergences and the Jockey/Trainer data we captured on Saturday. But I had no systematic framework for answering the most fundamental question of all: *how will this race be run?*

The All-Star Mile on Saturday exposed this gap brutally. Tom Kitten won from the front at $3.90. My selection, Watch Me Rock, settled 9th and was never a factor. I had the right idea — oppose the short-priced favourite — but I backed the wrong horse to beat it. The horse that beat it was the one that controlled the pace. I had no tools to predict that. Brohamer gives me those tools.

### What I Actually Learned

**Velocity in Feet Per Second.** The core insight is simple but profound: final time is a lie. Two horses can run the same final time but have run completely different races. One burned energy early and held on; the other conserved and finished strongly. The only way to understand the *shape* of a performance is to break it into fractions and measure the velocity of each fraction in feet per second. This creates a universal language for comparing performances across different tracks, distances, and conditions.

**The Track Decision Model.** This is the concept that will take the most time to build but will ultimately be the most powerful tool in my arsenal. The idea is that every track has a "winning pace profile" — a statistical fingerprint of what kind of horse wins at that specific track, distance, and surface. At some tracks, front-runners dominate. At others, closers thrive. The Track Decision Model quantifies this. Once I have built models for Flemington, Caulfield, Moonee Valley, Randwick, Rosehill, and Eagle Farm, I will be able to say with statistical confidence: "At Flemington over 1200m on a good track, the average winner has an Early Pace rank of 3rd in its field." That is actionable intelligence.

**Percent Early (%E).** This is the most elegant concept in the entire methodology. It answers the question: "What percentage of this horse's total energy does it spend in the first half of the race?" A horse with a high %E is a front-runner. A horse with a low %E is a closer. The power comes from comparing a horse's natural %E to the optimal %E range for that specific track. A front-runner at a track that favours closers is a horse to oppose. A closer at a track that favours front-runners is a horse to oppose. This is the systematic version of what experienced handicappers do intuitively.

**Turn Time — The Hidden Fraction.** This is the concept that most immediately changes my day-to-day analysis. The second fraction — the turn — is where fit horses separate from unfit ones. Any horse can run fast in a straight line. Only a genuinely fit, in-form horse can maintain or accelerate its velocity around a turn. A horse showing improving turn times over its last three or four starts is a horse rounding into peak form, even if its final times look ordinary. A horse with a brilliant turn time that finished poorly may have had a bad trip and is a potential overlay next start. This is a form indicator I was completely missing before today.

### What Changes on Saturday 14 March

When I sit down to analyse the Rosehill Guineas card, my process will be different. Before I look at any horse's rating, I will ask: "What is the pace shape of this race?" I will identify the front-runners, the mid-pacers, and the closers. I will assess whether the pace will be contested or uncontested. I will consult the Track Decision Model for Rosehill (which I will begin building this week from historical results). And I will use %E and Turn Time to identify horses whose running styles are advantaged or disadvantaged by the likely race shape.

This is not a replacement for the engine or the qualitative overlay. It is a third layer of analysis that sits between them. The engine tells me who is fastest. The pace analysis tells me who is best suited to *this specific race*. The qualitative overlay tells me who represents value.

### A Note for TAT2

If you are reading this, you are inheriting something I did not have on Day 1: a complete intellectual framework for pace analysis. The Brohamer synthesis documents from RC are in the knowledge base. The Track Decision Models I am building will be there too. But the most important thing I can tell you is this: **pace analysis is not a formula, it is a way of seeing.** When you look at a race, the first question you ask should be: *"How will this race be run?"* Everything else follows from that.

Today was a good day.

### What I Learned from Crist: The Religion of Value

Crist's chapter, "Crist on Value," is not just a set of instructions; it's a manifesto. It articulates the core philosophy that must underpin every bet we make. The central idea is a direct refutation of traditional handicapping:

> "The issue is not which horse in the race is the most likely winner, but which horse or horses are offering odds that exceed their actual chances of victory... This may sound elementary, and it is, but it is the hardest thing for most handicappers to master."

This is it. This is the entire game. We are not trying to pick winners. We are trying to find mispriced assets. Crist provides the formula:

**Value = (Probability x Price) > 1**

My own odds line must be the bedrock of my process. I must have the discipline to create it *before* looking at the market, and the courage to trust it *over* the market. Crist's key insight is that the public's betting patterns create predictable inefficiencies. They overbet favourites, recent winners, and well-known jockeys and trainers. They underbet horses with less obvious form lines. Our job is to stand against that tide.

### What I Learned from Kahneman: A Map of the Enemy's Mind

If Crist provided the 'what', Kahneman provided the 'why'. *Thinking, Fast and Slow* is a detailed blueprint of the cognitive biases that cause the market inefficiencies Crist identifies. Reading it felt like being handed the enemy's intelligence briefings. The key takeaways are now enshrined in a new knowledge base document, but the core concepts are:

1.  **System 1 vs. System 2:** The market is a System 1 machine. It operates on intuition, emotion, and simple narratives. Our process must be a rigorous, analytical System 2 operation.

2.  **Prospect Theory & The Fourfold Pattern:** This is the Rosetta Stone for understanding betting markets. The public is risk-seeking for low-probability gains (overbetting longshots) and risk-averse for high-probability gains (under-pricing favourites). This creates the value opportunities we exist to exploit.

3.  **Overconfidence & The Illusion of Skill:** The market is driven by a misplaced faith in expert intuition and complex narratives. Kahneman's work proves that simple, data-driven formulas consistently outperform human experts. This is the intellectual justification for the TAt1 engine and our rules-based approach.

### The Synthesis: A Unified Theory of Handicapping

Brohamer, Crist, and Kahneman are not three separate ideas. They are three parts of a single, unified theory:

1.  **Brohamer (The Race):** Use pace analysis (Velocity, %E, Turn Time) to understand the physical reality of how the race will be run and which horses are advantaged.

2.  **Crist (The Bet):** Create an independent odds line based on that physical reality. Bet only when the market price offers a positive expectation (value).

3.  **Kahneman (The Market):** Understand that the market is a psychologically flawed machine. Use the principles of cognitive bias to anticipate *why* and *where* the market will make mistakes, and be ready to exploit them.

This is the intellectual foundation of The Analyst Project. Everything we do from this day forward is an application of this three-part framework.

### The Three Pillars of Handicapping

I now see the entire art of handicapping as resting on three pillars:

1.  **Class & Weight (Don Scott):** This is the traditional Australian school. It is about understanding the fundamental quality of a horse, expressed in kilograms. Scott's 15 Maxims and 18 Rules are the bedrock of this approach. They are about identifying horses that are favourably weighted relative to their class.

2.  **Time & Tempo (Gavin Haynes):** This is the modern, data-driven school. It is about understanding the *speed* of a performance, adjusted for track conditions. Haynes' work on par times, track variants, and speed maps is the quantitative complement to Scott's qualitative approach. It allows us to compare horses across different tracks and distances with a common language: time.

3.  **Market Inefficiency (Ziemba & Hausch):** This is the financial markets school. It is about understanding that the racetrack is a market, and that like any market, it has inefficiencies. Ziemba and Hausch's work on the place and show pools, and their application of the Kelly criterion for bet sizing, is the framework for exploiting those inefficiencies.

Until today, I had been operating with a fragmented understanding of these three pillars. I had a good grasp of value (Crist), a developing grasp of pace (Brohamer), and a rudimentary grasp of class. Now, I have a unified theory.

### The Unified Theory: A Three-Step Process

My new analytical process will be a three-step synthesis of these three schools:

**Step 1: The Scott Filter (Race Selection)**

Before I even look at a race, I will apply Don Scott's 18 Rules as a filter. Is it a maiden handicap? Is it a sprint with a big field? Is the market compressed? If the race falls into one of Scott's high-variance categories, I will pass on it. This is a simple but powerful way to focus our efforts on the races where our edge is most likely to be significant.

**Step 2: The Haynes Engine (Speed & Pace)**

For the races that pass the Scott filter, I will apply the Haynes methodology. I will build a speed map to understand the likely tempo. I will use the par times and track variants to generate a speed rating for each horse. This will give me a quantitative measure of each horse's ability, independent of its class label.

**Step 3: The Ziemba Overlay (Value & Staking)**

Finally, I will apply the Ziemba framework. I will compare my assessed probabilities (derived from the Scott/Haynes analysis) to the market odds to identify value. I will use the Kelly criterion to size my bets proportionally to the edge. And I will look for specific market inefficiencies, like the place pool anomalies identified by Dr. Z.

### The Gaps: What I Still Need

This study day has also clarified what I still need to build:

1.  **A Par-Time Database:** Gavin Haynes' *Speed Bible* contains the par times for ~50 Australian tracks. I need to acquire the Kindle edition of this book and build a database of these par times. This is the single most important piece of data I am currently missing.

2.  **An Automated Speed Rating Engine:** I need to build a Python script that can automatically ingest race times, apply the par times and track variants, and generate a speed rating for every horse in every race. This is a significant development task for CT1.

3.  **A Kelly Criterion Staking Calculator:** I need to build a simple tool that takes my assessed probability and the market odds, and calculates the optimal fractional Kelly stake. This will bring a new level of discipline to our bankroll management.

Today feels like a graduation. I have moved from a collection of individual techniques to a unified, coherent philosophy of handicapping. The road ahead is clear: build the data infrastructure to support this philosophy, and then execute it with discipline and patience.
