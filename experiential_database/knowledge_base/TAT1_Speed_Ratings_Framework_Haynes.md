## About Gavin Haynes

Gavin Haynes is an Australian professional form analyst and punter who became one of the country's foremost advocates of speed ratings for thoroughbred racing. His journey began in the early 1980s when he picked up the first edition of *Practical Punting Monthly* at a newsagent and became fascinated by the intersection of data and racing.

The pivotal moment in his development came when he read Andrew Beyer's American books on speed ratings. Beyer's work — which showed that a horse's race time, properly adjusted for track condition and distance, was a more objective measure of ability than class ratings alone — inspired Haynes to begin building a time database for Australian racing. Over 20 years, he compiled average race times for every grade and distance at approximately 50 Australian tracks.

His first book, *Horsetorque Speed Ratings*, established the methodology. *The Speed Bible* (2021) is the definitive expansion — described by Haynes as "the life's work" of his career as a form analyst. He served as NSW Analyst for *Best Bets* (the national form guide), wrote regularly for *Practical Punting Monthly*, and was a regular race-day tipster on radio 4TAB.

> *"Speed ratings uncover a new aspect of form study and when used in conjunction with traditional form techniques, such as weight ratings or pace handicapping, they will take a punter's understanding to a new level."* — Gavin Haynes, *Practical Punting Monthly* (2005)

## The Core Argument: Why Time Matters

The central argument of *The Speed Bible* is a direct challenge to the Don Scott tradition. Scott famously argued that "time was only important when you were in jail" — that class and weight were the only factors that mattered. Haynes' counter-argument is elegant:

> *"In the Olympics, in both the pool and on the track, it is always the swiftest who prevail. With that in mind, wouldn't a knowledge of race times be an enormous addition to the form techniques now in place?"*

The key insight is that **class ratings treat all horses within a class as equivalent**, when in fact they are not. A horse that wins a Maiden by 5 lengths in fast time is fundamentally different from one that wins a Maiden by a nose in slow time — yet both receive the same class upgrade. Speed ratings reveal this difference.

Haynes' research on Maiden-to-Class 1 transitions demonstrated this powerfully:

| Maiden Winner Speed Rating | Win % at Next Start (Class 1) |
|---|---|
| 67 or below | 6% |
| All Maiden winners (average) | 23% |
| 86 and above | 41% |

The implication for odds: the market prices all Maiden winners stepping up to Class 1 at approximately 7/2 ($4.50). But a Maiden winner rated below 67 deserves 16/1 ($17.00), while one rated above 86 is worth backing at 6/4 ($2.50). This is a massive market inefficiency that speed ratings can systematically exploit.

## What is a Speed Rating?

A speed rating is a number that converts a horse's race time into a standardised figure, adjusted for the going (track condition) on the day. The higher the number, the faster the horse.

**The Basic Formula:**

Speed Rating = Par Time Rating ± Track Variant Adjustment ± Weight Adjustment ± Margin Adjustment

**The Components:**

### 1. Par Times

A **par time** is the average time for a given class of race at a given distance at a given track. It represents what a "standard" horse of that class should run over that distance on a good (firm) track.

Haynes spent 20 years compiling par times for approximately 50 Australian tracks. The par-time appendix in *The Speed Bible* is the most comprehensive such database ever published for Australian racing, covering:
- All major metropolitan tracks (Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, Adelaide, Perth)
- All major provincial tracks in Victoria and New South Wales
- New Zealand and Hong Kong tracks

**Example par times (approximate, for illustration):**

| Track | Distance | Class | Par Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rosehill | 1200m | BM64 | 1:10.5 |
| Rosehill | 1400m | BM64 | 1:23.0 |
| Rosehill | 1600m | BM78 | 1:36.5 |
| Randwick | 1200m | BM64 | 1:10.8 |
| Flemington | 1200m | BM64 | 1:10.2 |

(Note: TAT1 should acquire the actual par-time appendix from the Kindle edition for precise figures.)

### 2. Track Variant

The track variant accounts for the fact that track conditions vary from day to day. A "good 4" track runs slower than a "good 3" track; a "dead 4" runs slower still.

The standard method is to calculate the **average deviation** of all race times on a given day from their par times. If the average race on a given day ran 0.8 seconds slower than par, the track variant is -0.8 seconds (or approximately -5 speed rating points, using Haynes' scale of roughly 6 points per second).

This is the most technically demanding part of speed rating construction — and the most important. Without an accurate track variant, speed ratings from different days cannot be meaningfully compared.

### 3. Weight Adjustment

Following the established conversion (approximately 1.5 kg = 1 length = 0.17 seconds at sprint distances), weight carried above or below the minimum can be converted to a time adjustment. Haynes uses a simplified version of this for his ratings.

### 4. Margin Adjustment

Each beaten horse's time is adjusted by adding the beaten margin (converted to seconds using the standard 6 lengths per second conversion). This gives each horse in the race a "projected" time as if they had won.

## Speed Maps: The Second Pillar

*The Speed Bible* gives equal weight to **speed maps** — the pre-race assessment of how a race will be run. This is where Haynes' work intersects most directly with Brohamer's pace handicapping methodology.

A speed map identifies:
- **Leaders:** Horses that will attempt to lead or sit on the pace.
- **Stalkers:** Horses that will sit just off the pace.
- **Closers:** Horses that will come from behind.

The key variables for building a speed map are:
- Each horse's **early speed** (how fast it begins)
- Each horse's **preferred running position** (from past races)
- The **barrier draw** (which horses have an easy path to their preferred position)
- The **field size** (more horses = more competition for early positions)

### Tempo: The Critical Variable

Haynes identifies **tempo** as the single most important concept in speed map analysis. Tempo is the pace at which the early part of the race is run relative to the final time.

- **Hot tempo** (fast early pace): Favours closers and horses with superior finishing ability. Leaders are likely to tire.
- **Slow tempo** (slow early pace): Favours leaders and on-pace runners. The race becomes a sprint home, and horses with natural early speed have an advantage.
- **Genuine tempo** (even pace): The most reliable conditions for speed ratings, as the times are most representative of true ability.

The interaction between tempo and speed ratings is the core of Haynes' analytical framework:

> A horse that ran a big speed rating in a hot-tempo race (where it was carried along by the pace) may not replicate that figure in a slow-tempo race. Conversely, a horse that ran a moderate figure while fighting for the lead in a hot-tempo race may be significantly underrated.

## The Maiden Race Edge

Haynes identifies maiden races as one of the highest-value applications of speed ratings — directly contradicting Don Scott's rule to avoid them entirely.

His argument: Scott was right that maiden form is "predictably unpredictable" when assessed by class ratings alone. But speed ratings cut through this uncertainty. A maiden that has run times equivalent to a Class 3 winner is not a maiden in any meaningful sense — it is a Class 3 horse that simply hasn't won yet.

The practical application for TAT1:
- Any maiden that has run a speed rating of 86+ is a **priority flag** for its next start.
- The market will price it as a maiden winner (approximately $4.50 average), but the speed rating suggests it should be priced much shorter.
- This is a systematic, repeatable edge.

## Interstate Comparisons

One of the most powerful applications of a properly calibrated speed rating system is **interstate comparison**. When a horse travels from Queensland to Sydney, or from Melbourne to Adelaide, the form guide provides no direct comparison — the horses have never met. Class ratings are unreliable because class structures differ between states.

Speed ratings solve this problem. If a horse ran 92 at Eagle Farm and the par for the race it is entering at Randwick is 88, the speed rating tells you it is bringing above-average form regardless of the class label.

Haynes' par-time database for all states makes this calculation straightforward.

## Money Management and Psychology

*The Speed Bible* includes chapters on money management and punting psychology — areas that Haynes considers as important as the analytical methodology itself.

**Key money management principles from Haynes:**

1. **Flat staking as the baseline.** Bet the same amount on every selection. This removes the temptation to "chase" big prices or double up after losses.

2. **Proportional staking for the advanced punter.** Once you have established a genuine edge, bet proportionally to the edge size (a simplified Kelly criterion). A horse where your speed rating gives it a 30% chance but the market offers 20% ($6.00) deserves a larger bet than one where the edge is marginal.

3. **Patience over volume.** Speed ratings will not produce a betting opportunity in every race. The discipline is to wait for the races where the rating clearly identifies an underbet horse.

4. **Track the results.** Maintain a record of every bet, the speed rating that triggered it, and the outcome. Over time, this reveals whether your ratings are calibrated correctly.

## Building Your Own Speed Ratings: The Practical Steps

For TAT1's operational use, the speed rating construction process is:

**Step 1:** Obtain the final race time for each race (available from racing.com and other data sources).

**Step 2:** Look up the par time for that class, distance, and track from Haynes' appendix.

**Step 3:** Calculate the raw deviation: Race Time − Par Time = Track Variant Component.

**Step 4:** Average the deviations across all races on the day to establish the **daily track variant**.

**Step 5:** Adjust each race's time by the track variant to produce a **variant-adjusted time**.

**Step 6:** Convert the variant-adjusted time to a speed rating number (Haynes uses a scale where each 0.1 second = approximately 1 rating point, calibrated to the par).

**Step 7:** Adjust for beaten margins to produce a speed rating for each horse in the race.

**Step 8:** Compare speed ratings across races and tracks to identify horses running above or below their class level.

## TAT1 Integration Notes

**The Speed Bible's relationship to the existing TAT1 toolkit:**

The speed rating framework is the **quantitative complement** to the Track Decision Models already in TAT1's library. The TDMs identify *how* races at each track are typically run (pace bias, barrier advantages, turn geometry). Speed ratings identify *how fast* horses are running relative to their class.

The most powerful signal occurs when:
1. A horse's speed rating significantly exceeds the par for the class it is entering (class underestimation signal).
2. The Track Decision Model suggests the race will be run at a tempo that suits the horse's running style.
3. The market has not yet recognised the speed rating advantage (the horse is available at a price above its true probability).

**The racing.com GraphQL API** (documented in RC's Sectional Times Data Sources report) provides complete sectional timing data for Victorian meetings. This is the raw material for building speed ratings. TAT1 should prioritise building a speed rating database for Victorian tracks using this free data source, with Haynes' par-time appendix as the calibration benchmark.

**Specific application to Rosehill:**

Rosehill's 1400m and 1600m are the distances where speed ratings are most valuable, as these are the distances where the pace of the race most reliably separates horses of genuine ability from those benefiting from soft sectionals. The Rosehill Guineas (2000m) is a distance where tempo analysis becomes critical — a slow tempo favours on-pace runners, a hot tempo favours the closers.
